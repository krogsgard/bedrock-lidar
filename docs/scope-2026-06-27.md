## 0. Executive Summary

**What's being scoped.** A cloud-native SaaS that takes raw drone lidar (10–100 GB .las/.laz), produces a best-in-class ground classification specifically for **heavy-vegetation tracts**, lets you QC the result in a browser, and exports clean deliverables into LP360 / Carlson / Civil 3D / TBC. Built first for Weygand, designed to be sold to other surveying firms.

**Is it feasible in 2026?** Yes — every piece is buildable with open source or commodity infra. The ML literature (PTv3, KPConv-X, SuperPoint Transformer, Sonata self-supervised pretraining) has matured to where a small team can train a model that beats CSF/PMF/TerraScan on dense canopy. The cloud rendering stack (COPC + CesiumJS/Potree v2) is finally streaming-native.

**Is there a moat?** One real one: **a proprietary labeled dataset of Southeast US heavy-vegetation lidar tracts, captured by Weygand's own flights, corrected by your own surveyors.** Nobody else has it and nobody else can build it without your sensors and your terrain. Every other angle (cloud-native, browser QC, per-tract pricing, COPC-first) is execution, not IP.

**Will it compete?** Honest answer:
- **Internal-only ROI is positive on its own.** 15–40 hrs/job of cleanup at ~$75/hr loaded means a working tool pays for itself within ~30 jobs.
- **Regional SaaS (5–30 SE-US firms, $60–160K ARR)** is the realistic upside.
- **National SaaS or acquisition target** is achievable but requires a real second hire and 2–4 years.
- **GeoCue is moving.** They announced AI Ground+ and AI Forestry add-ons at Geo Week 2026. The window to differentiate is 18–36 months before incumbents catch up on heavy-vegetation specifically.

**Total to a usable v1 internal tool**: ~6 months of focused work + ~$500–2,500 in GPU training spend + 5–15 carefully labeled tracts.
**Total to a multi-tenant SaaS with billing**: ~12–18 months, ~$30–50K in cloud + tooling for the build phase, plus a labeling investment of $5–20K in surveyor time.

The single highest-leverage exercise is **Phase 0**: take three real Weygand flights, push them through a PDAL + Cesium spike in two weeks, and measure the time saved against the LP360 baseline. That answers the build/no-build question with real numbers instead of speculation.

## 1. The Problem We're Solving

Every other lidar tool already classifies ground "well enough" in open terrain. The brutal failure mode — the one that costs your surveyors hours per job and forces manual cleanup in LP360 — is **dense canopy with sparse ground returns**:

- Mixed Southeast hardwood/pine
- Pine plantation with understory
- Cypress swamp
- Laurel / rhododendron thickets
- Kudzu

In those conditions, per-pulse ground return rates can drop below 1 pt/m² even at 60 pts/m² aggregate density. Geometric filters (CSF, PMF, Axelsson's TIN-densification that powers TerraScan) all share the same failure mode there: they assume "lowest local point = ground." When the lowest local point is actually a root buttress or low understory, the DTM floats 0.5–2 m high and looks like a checkerboard of bumps.

Modern ML semantic segmentation models, given proper training data, see this case differently. They learn the **multi-scale geometric signature** of ground vs. understory vs. canopy, and they get 5–15% IoU improvement on dense forest specifically — which translates to dramatically less manual cleanup downstream.

**The product is built around that one job: find the ground reliably under heavy vegetation, fast, in the cloud, with the QC and export experience a working surveyor expects.**

## 2. Product Vision

A working surveyor uploads a flight. Twenty minutes later they're in a browser, looking at their classified point cloud over satellite basemap and terrain. They can:

1. **See it immediately.** Stream the full cloud — 100M to 1B+ points — at 60 fps from R2 with no desktop install.
2. **Trust it, but verify.** Side-by-side view of "what the ML model thinks" vs. "what the geometric filter thinks." Disagreement regions auto-flagged. Confidence heatmap.
3. **Edit it surveyor-style.** Brush, polygon select, profile slice, height filter. All operations append-only, undoable, audit-logged (PLS will sign this).
4. **Export everything.** LAS 1.4 with ASPRS classes, COPC for re-hosting, GeoTIFF DTM, SHP contours, DWG breaklines, E57 if needed. Hot-folder to a customer's Drive, REST API push, or download bundle.
5. **Share with a client.** A read-only viewer URL that opens on the client's iPad on a job site.

For Weygand internally, this replaces the 15–40 hrs of LP360 cleanup per heavy-canopy tract. For SaaS customers, this is "cloud-native LP360 alternative built by surveyors, optimized for vegetation."

## 4. The ML Approach — How We Beat the Status Quo

### Architecture pick

After surveying the 2024–2026 literature (PointNet++, RandLA-Net, KPConv/KPConv-X, MinkowskiNet, PointNeXt, Stratified Transformer, Point Transformer v1/v2/v3, SuperPoint Transformer, Sonata, PPT), the recommended stack is:

1. **Primary backbone: Point Transformer v3 (PTv3)**, fine-tuned from a **Sonata self-supervised pretrained checkpoint**.
   - PTv3 is current state-of-the-art on essentially every outdoor benchmark (DALES, Toronto3D, SensatUrban, Waymo, SemanticKITTI).
   - Sonata pretraining cuts labeling needs by 3–10× — a critical lever for a small team.
   - Both live in the **Pointcept** library — the most important point-cloud segmentation repo in the field as of 2026.

2. **Fast-path fallback: SuperPoint Transformer (SPT)**.
   - 200× fewer parameters than PTv3 (212K vs ~40M).
   - Trains on DALES in 3 hours on a single A100.
   - Inference on a full 1 km² ALS tile in under 30 seconds.
   - Cost-attractive for the per-tract economics; also serves as a sanity-check against the heavier model.

3. **Always run CSF in parallel** as the geometric baseline. Any region where ML and CSF disagree by >5% is auto-flagged for human QC. This is **the safety net** that makes the system trustworthy enough for a PLS to sign — and also feeds the continuous active-learning loop.

### Comparative model table

| Model | DALES Ground IoU | Inference throughput (A100) | Train time | Fine-tuning ease |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| PointNet++ | ~93 | ~100k pts/s | 4–12h | Easy (baseline only) |
| RandLA-Net | ~95 | 1–2M pts/s | 6–12h | Very easy |
| PointNeXt | ~96 | 400k–1M pts/s | 6–15h | Very easy |
| KPConv-X (2024) | ~97 | 700k–1.5M pts/s | 8–20h | Excellent |
| MinkowskiNet | ~96 | 1–3M pts/s | 8–20h | Moderate (engine pain) |
| **PTv3** | **~97+** | **500k–1.5M pts/s** | 24–72h (fine-tune 2–6h) | **Excellent (Pointcept)** |
| **SuperPoint Transformer** | ~96 | **~3–5M pts/s effective** | **3h** | **Excellent** |
| Sonata (pretraining) | n/a | n/a | one-time | **Reduces label needs 3–10×** |

### Training data strategy

The labeling investment is the largest single cost. Reasonable numbers:

- **Fine-tuning Sonata-pretrained PTv3 for ground vs non-ground:** 5–30M labeled points = **5–15 ha of careful labeling** = 2–4 weeks of one surveyor's time. Achievable.
- **Diversity is the key word.** Don't label 50 tiles all from pine plantation. Stratify across canopy types: open pasture, agricultural, suburban mature canopy, hardwood, pine, mixed, swamp, riparian. Hit at least 2–3 tiles each.
- **Smart labeling pipeline:** Run CSF first, then have a surveyor correct the vegetation areas in TerraScan/LP360 (tools they already know), export corrected classification. The CSF output gives a 95% head start; you're effectively labeling the corrections, not the whole tile.
- **Synthetic augmentation:** Use **Helios++** (open-source aerial lidar simulator) over procedural forest scenes (Blender Sapling) at varied canopy closure 60–95%. Pretrain on synthetic, fine-tune on real. Several papers (Winiwarter 2022) show this matches real-only training.
- **Pseudo-labeling + active learning loop:** CSF-confident regions become free labels. Tiles with high predictive entropy get prioritized for human review. Realistic 2–3× labeling efficiency gain.

## 5. Cloud Architecture

### Storage format: COPC, full stop

COPC (Cloud Optimized Point Cloud) has won the streaming format war. By 2024 it was adopted by USGS 3DEP, OpenTopography, every major drone vendor's cloud product, and the LAStools toolchain. **A COPC file is also a valid LAZ 1.4 file** — any survey software that reads LAZ reads it directly, zero migration cost.

Avoid: EPT (thousands of small sidecar files, painful on object storage, declining), Potree v2 octree (visualization-only), proprietary tile formats.

For visualization output, optionally publish 3D Tiles 1.1 alongside the COPC for Cesium-native rendering.

### Two-renderer strategy

| Surface | Library | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Public / customer-facing viewer** (share-a-link, iPad on jobsite) | **CesiumJS + 3D Tiles** | Only mature renderer that handles 1B+ points with terrain context and mobile. Customer opens link, sees their site over satellite + Cesium World Terrain. |
| **Power-user QC editor** (in-house techs) | **Potree v2** (or fork) + **copc.js** | Built-in profile tool, height filter, classification brush. Power users already know it. Loads COPC directly, no separate tile build. |

**Do not** build a custom Three.js renderer from scratch. 12–18 months of reinventing octree streaming. Three.js makes sense only if you outgrow Cesium and Potree — won't happen for a surveying SaaS.

### Compute pattern (recommended for Weygand)

**Hybrid: R2 storage + DigitalOcean baseline GPU + Modal burst.**

```
                   ┌───────────────────────────────────┐
                   │ trymay.ai / weygand.com (CF Pages)│
                   │  - Upload UI (resumable, R2)      │
                   │  - QC viewer (CesiumJS + Potree)  │
                   └─────────────┬─────────────────────┘
                                 │
                  ┌──────────────▼───────────────┐
                  │  Cloudflare R2 (storage)      │
                  │  /uploads/<job>/raw.laz       │
                  │  /jobs/<job>/normalized.copc  │
                  │  /jobs/<job>/classified.copc  │
                  │  /jobs/<job>/dtm.tif          │
                  │  /jobs/<job>/edits.delta      │
                  └──────────────┬───────────────┘
                                 │
                  ┌──────────────▼───────────────┐
                  │  D1: job queue + state        │
                  └──────────────┬───────────────┘
                                 │
              ┌──────────────────┼──────────────────┐
              │                  │                  │
   ┌──────────▼────────┐ ┌───────▼──────┐ ┌─────────▼──────┐
   │ DO GPU droplet    │ │ Modal burst  │ │ DO CPU droplet │
   │ (L40S, always-on, │ │ (queue >2)   │ │ (Untwine COPC, │
   │ ML classify)      │ │              │ │ DTM, contours) │
   └───────────────────┘ └──────────────┘ └────────────────┘
```

**Why this stack:**
- You already operate DigitalOcean — no new vendor relationship. One always-on L40S handles steady-state.
- Modal handles burst (5 jobs hit at once, ~10s cold-start on warm pool, pay-per-second).
- **R2 zero egress** means the browser can stream a 50GB COPC directly to CesiumJS with no per-GB charge. This is the structural margin advantage.
- D1 as queue is fine to ~100 concurrent jobs.

### Avoid

- **AWS Lambda / Cloudflare Workers for processing.** 15-min cap and 30s CPU cap respectively, plus body size limits. They are perfect for the control plane (job submit, status, auth) but actively wrong for 100GB jobs.
- **Pure Kubernetes** until you're at 50+ jobs/day. Ops burden vs. DO droplets + Modal is not worth it.
- **LAStools server-side.** License entanglement, single-vendor risk. PDAL closes the gap.

## 6. QC Editing — The Data Model That Makes It Work

**Never rewrite the source COPC during QC.** Edits are stored as a sparse delta layer, merged at render and export time. This is what unlocks fast undo, audit trail, multi-user, and 100× cheaper storage I/O than rewriting tiles.

### Layout

```
/jobs/<job>/classified.copc           (immutable, ML output)
/jobs/<job>/edits/
    v1.delta.parquet                   (Parquet: point_id, old_class, new_class, op_id, user, ts)
    v2.delta.parquet
    ops.jsonl                          (high-level edit ops: polygon coords, brush, etc.)
```

Parquet for the deltas means a 10M-point edit set is ~40MB, R2-range-readable, and the renderer can fetch only the deltas for the current octree node.

### Render-time merge

When the viewer requests a COPC chunk, a worker streams the chunk from R2, fetches the matching delta slice, and applies overrides in-memory before sending to GPU. Sub-10ms overhead per chunk.

### Export-time merge

When the user exports final .las/.laz, a PDAL pipeline reads source COPC + delta files and writes a new LAS with merged classifications. This is the **commit** — produces the clean deliverable that the PLS signs.

### Operation log (ops.jsonl)

Stores the high-level user action — polygon coords, brush radius, target class, user, timestamp. Critical for QC sign-off: a state surveyor wants to know who reclassified what, and when. This is also free training data for the next ML iteration.

### Edit primitives to ship

| Primitive | v1 | v2 |
|---|---|---|
| Brush (radius-based reclassify) | ✓ | |
| Polygon select (plan or profile view) | ✓ | |
| Single-point pick | ✓ | |
| Undo / redo (free, edits are ordered ops) | ✓ | |
| Height filter ("all points >2.5m above ground in this region → veg") | | ✓ |
| Profile slice editor (lifted from Potree's profile tool — hardest UX) | | ✓ |

## 7. Export — Plays Nicely With Every CAD/Survey Sink

Every completed job auto-generates:

1. **`<job>.las` (LAS 1.4, ASPRS classes)** — full classified point cloud. Universal.
2. **`<job>_dtm.tif` (Cloud Optimized GeoTIFF)** — bare-earth DTM at configurable resolution (1ft/30cm typical). PDAL `writers.gdal` with IDW or Delaunay.
3. **`<job>_contours.shp`** — vector contours at configurable interval (1ft, 2ft, 5ft). `gdal_contour`.
4. **`<job>_breaklines.dwg`** — hard breaklines. ezdxf for DWG/DXF write.
5. **`<job>.e57`** — for Trimble/Leica scanning workflows. PDAL `writers.e57`.

### Integration patterns

- **Hot folder / Google Drive sync** — write bundle to customer-specific Drive folder. Reuse Weygand's existing Drive token broker (`setup-token.env`). Surveyors are accustomed to "check the Drive folder, files appear when done."
- **REST API** for power users — `GET /api/jobs/<id>/exports/{las|dtm|contours|dwg|e57}`.
- **Resend digest email** — link to download bundle when export completes.

### Differentiator opportunity

**Direct push into Trimble Connect via their API** — Trimble Connect has a documented API, almost no competitor has automated this. Most surveyors still drag-drop manually. A "Send to TBC" button would be a real differentiator for the Trimble-shop subsegment.

## 8. Honest Risks & Open Questions

### Technical risks
- **ML accuracy on Southeast US terrain is unproven** until you label 5–15 ha and train. The literature says 94–99% is achievable; your specific terrain mix is the test.
- **GPU spot capacity for L40S/A10/L4 tightened in 2025–2026** as inference demand exploded. DigitalOcean has been more reliable than AWS for L40S in 2026 — but keep a baseline always-on so you never starve.
- **CesiumJS licensing is drifting commercial-first** under Cesium GS / Bentley. OSS core is still strong but watch every 6 months.
- **COPC writer maturity at >1B points.** Untwine has shipped fixes in 2024–2026 but multi-flight merged datasets (3–5B points) still occasionally need manual chunking.

### Strategic risks
- **GeoCue's AI Ground+ and AI Forestry** are aimed at exactly this wedge. They have hundreds of engineers, install base, and dealer channels. 18–36 month window before they close the gap on heavy vegetation.
- **Distribution to small surveying firms is slow.** Conservative buyers, long sales cycles, reference-customer flywheel takes 2–3 years. A 3-person Birmingham firm can't outspend GeoCue on conference booths or training.
- **Per-tract pricing economics are tight at low volume.** Lidarvisor at $89/mo struggles with margins; you would too in year 1.
- **The "internal tool" trap.** Most "we built it for ourselves" SaaS attempts never become real SaaS — productization, support, billing, and onboarding are 5× the engineering work, and surveying firms make tough customers (low volume, high support).

### Realistic outcome distribution

|---|---|---|---|
| Internal tool only | 50% | 50–70% reduction in processing time per project; faster turnaround wins more jobs | $50–150K/yr labor savings. Low risk. |

### Moats, honestly ranked

1. **Proprietary labeled dataset of SE-US heavy-vegetation tracts** — the only real long-term moat. Compounds with every job processed.
2. **Surveyor-built brand + PLS-stamped deliverables** — distribution moat. Trust takes years to build, hard for outsiders to fake.

## 10. Recommended Phase 0 — Two Weeks, Real Data, Real Decision

The single highest-leverage exercise is taking three real Weygand drone deliverables and pushing them through a PDAL + Cesium spike. You'll learn more in those two weeks than in another month of research:

1. **Pick three tracts** spanning your worst-case canopy diversity (one open, one moderate mixed, one heavy dense pine/hardwood).
2. **Build the PDAL pipeline**: ingest LAZ → SMRF/CSF classify → write COPC → upload to R2.
3. **Stand up a CesiumJS viewer** at `trymay.ai/spike` (or `lidar.weygand.com`) that loads the COPC.
4. **Run the same three tracts through LP360** as your current baseline. Measure: wall-clock processing time, manual cleanup hours, final DTM RMSE against your control points.
5. **Compare**:
   - How does CSF alone (without ML) do on each tract?
   - How much extra manual time would the LP360 baseline have required to match the CSF output?
   - What does the COPC + Cesium streaming experience feel like?
6. **Write a one-page summary** with the numbers. **Decision gate:** does the time-savings delta justify the Phase 1 investment?

If Phase 0 says yes, the rest of the plan unlocks. If it says no, you've spent two weeks and learned the real answer instead of speculating.

## Appendix A — Key Open-Source Repos to Anchor On

- `Pointcept/Pointcept` — PTv3, PTv2, KPConv, MinkowskiNet, OctFormer in one PyTorch codebase. **The most important repo in this space.**
- `Pointcept/Sonata` — self-supervised pretraining backbone, 3–10× labeling reduction.
- `drprojects/superpoint_transformer` — SPT, 200× fewer params, 3-hour training.
- `HuguesTHOMAS/KPConvX` — KPConv-X (2024 modernized rewrite).
- `QingyongHu/RandLA-Net` — efficient baseline, Toronto3D checkpoint.
- `guochengqian/openpoints` — PointNeXt + unified codebase for older architectures.
- `PDAL/PDAL` — the universal point-cloud processing pipeline.
- `hobu/untwine` — official COPC writer.
- `3dgeo-heidelberg/helios` — open-source aerial lidar simulator (synthetic training data).
- `CloudCompare/CloudCompare` — viewer + labeling.
- `potree/potree` — browser viewer baseline.

## Appendix B — Datasets to Cite or Mine

- **DALES** — 505M point ALS, 8 classes (Dayton, OH). The aerial-lidar benchmark.
- **OpenGF** — 542M point ground-filter benchmark, 9 terrain scenes. Most directly relevant.
- **LASDU** — large-scale ALS urban, 5 classes including ground.
- **Toronto3D** — 78M point mobile mapping, 8 classes.
- **Hessigheim 3D** — UAV-borne high-density, 11 classes. Closest to drone use case.
- **SensatUrban** — large-scale UAV photogrammetry. Recent benchmark.
- **USGS 3DEP** — public US national lidar, includes classified ground in many tiles. Free training data.
